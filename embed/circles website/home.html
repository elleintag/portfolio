Team-based mentoring for foster youth in the Greater Toronto Area

We aim to see youth in the foster system thrive by experiencing the care and support of a long-term community who is unconditionally “for” them up to and beyond their 18th birthday

A Team Approach to Mentorship

At Circles, our mentoring relationships are not governed by a time limit, a goal met, or professional obligation. Our teams of mentors are simply people who show up consistently and truly enjoy time with their youth. No pressure. Simply presence.

58% of youth leaving for foster care each year experience homelessness.

Most youth in foster care grow up knowing that many of their key supports and relationships, (including with their foster family), will end at some point between ages 18 to 21, a process referred to as “aging out”. Yet stable, reliable, safe, and trustworthy relationships with adults are a key protective factor against many challenges faced by youth exiting foster care, and in particular reduces their chance of becoming homeless when they age out (Kovarikova, “Exploring Youth Outcomes After Aging-Out of Care,” 2017).

I developed a sense of abandonment because nobody really wants me. I’m in care, care kicks me out and they don’t [want me], and nobody calls me, I don’t even have a phone, I get $50 a month and nobody [cares] about it.

Arlo, age 24 from "Costs of State Guardianship in Ontario"

(I remember) sitting in my room alone the day I turned 21. I’d been alone a million times in that room before, but I’d never really felt alone like I did at that point in time. 

Justine, age 25, former youth in care (from My Real Life Book: Report From The Youth Leaving Care Hearings, 2011)

No other relationship begins with an expiry date.

Adam, age 27, former youth in care (from My Real Life Book: Report From The Youth Leaving Care Hearings, 2011)

The importance of lifelong connections with adults who can offer emotional support over the long term is critical. For many youth in care, leaving care is when they struggle the most — and are therefore in the greatest need of consistency, stability, love, and guidance

(“Permanency,” Ontario Association of Children’s Aid Societies)."

Even one close contact or merely the perception of having social support available decreases risk of homelessness significantly

(Kovarikova, “Exploring Youth Outcomes After Aging-Out of Care,” 2017).

Having a positive support team, environment and structure, leads to positive outcomes. Being vulnerable is a natural part of life. The main difference between the vulnerability of a child in care and a child at home, is the support system and environment they live in. Children and youth in care lead a rollercoaster life.

Alicia, age 23, former youth in care (from My Real Life Book: Report From The Youth Leaving Care Hearings, 2011)

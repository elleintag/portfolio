Our Mentorship Model

One Youth, Three Caring Adults

Circles utilizes a team model of mentorship. One youth is thoughtfully matched with a team of 3 adult mentors who connect weekly with the youth around a fun and meaningful activity. Over time, and through consistent, intentional engagement, the team of mentors show themselves to be people the youth can trust, confide in, and celebrate with. In other words, Circles provides an experience of a healthy community who will be present and supportive as the youth ages out of the foster care system.

Become a Mentor

CONSISTENCY

One defining feature of the experience of a youth in foster care is loss, and more specifically, relational loss. The team approach to mentoring provides consistency and stability even if a mentor has a scheduling conflict on a specific week. If a mentor cannot continue in their role, the youth will continue connecting with the other two mentors until a new mentor is found and welcomed into the team.

SUPPORT

By placing mentors within a team, they can learn from each other, lean on each other, and use each other’s strengths and experience for the benefit of the youth.

COMMUNITY

By connecting youth with three mentors, they will have access to a wider range of interests, experiences, and passions to learn from and about.

Become a Mentor

Supported by a Larger Community

Each mentorship team is embedded within a church community that supports and champions the mentors and youth. Mentors can draw on this wider “circle around the circle” to provide the youth with opportunities that go beyond the capability, capacity or resources of the mentorship team. The larger community also affords the youth the opportunity, if they so choose, to develop natural supports beyond their mentorship team that will enhance their long term well being.

This could look like:

Helping a youth move
Donating tickets to a sporting event or concert
Providing home necessities for a youth moving into their own space
Connecting youth with other professional and recreational opportunities

All of these opportunities are offered in a way that keeps the youth safe, with no pressure or expectation that the youth join a church or agree with a particular set of beliefs. We simply believe that youth benefit when they have as many diverse supports and opportunities as possible.

Training and Staff Support

Each mentor undergoes 8-12 hours of training before becoming part of a team and connecting with a youth. This training covers relevant topics such as the foster care system in Ontario, the impact of developmental trauma, how to listen and ask good questions, and information around equity, diversity, and inclusion.

In addition, each mentor team receives ongoing support from a trained and experienced staff member. This staff member provides consistent check ins, troubleshooting and mediation if necessary, and is the liaison between the youth and any other professional support they may have.
